Video Game Addiction

Having first been seen in the early 60s, video gaming began to see mainstream success in the early 70s and 80s, with the advent of the home console and video arcade. In the past few decades, however, with the rise in consumer adoption of home gaming, and the increasing affordability of both PC and console gaming, video game addiction has been seen as a more and more pressing concern amongst today's youth.

As arguably one of the newest forms of media, the long term effects of video games on the developing brain has still yet to be fully studied. That said, concerns over addiction (as well as games' correlation with violent/antisocial tendencies) among consumers of video games has been a topic of much discussion in media in recent years—as far back as the late 80s/early 90s, with the steady rise in graphical capabilities in video gaming consoles.

With the exponential growth in computing capabilities and continuous rise in video game popularity/ease of access—especially with the push towards fully digital games marketplaces, such as Valve's Steam, EA's Origin, or even Sony/Microsoft/Nintendo's own "e-stores"—these concerns are more relevant than ever, especially considering the wider demographic that games have seen in recent years.

In this site, I am to look into the concerns surrounding video game addiction, compare statistics of video game "addiction" amongst youth/adolescent populations, and propose potential fixes to concerns regaring VG Addiction.
